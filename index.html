01. The 9 Laws

Module 5 focuses on manipulating logical statements. You don't need to memorize complex formulas right away; you need to understand what they do conceptually. Click the eye icon to test yourself on translations.

Idempotent

Repeated execution

p ∧ p ≡ p
p ∨ p ≡ p
Eng: "It is raining AND it is raining" → It is raining.

Commutative

Order is irrelevant

p ∧ q ≡ q ∧ p
p ∨ q ≡ q ∨ p
p ↔ q ≡ q ↔ p
Eng: "I have a cat AND a dog" ≡ "I have a dog AND a cat"

Associative

Parenthesis shift

(p∧q)∧r ≡ p∧(q∧r)
(p∨q)∨r ≡ p∨(q∨r)
(p↔q)↔r ≡ p↔(q↔r)
Eng: "Wash car AND mow lawn, AND buy groceries" ≡ "Wash car, AND mow lawn AND buy groceries"

Distributive

Distribute over

p∧(q∨r) ≡ (p∧q)∨(p∧r)
p∨(q∧r) ≡ (p∨q)∧(p∨r)
Eng: "I will order a burger AND (either fries OR salad)"
Crucial Exam Topic

De Morgan's Laws

Negating a grouped AND turns it into an OR. Negating a grouped OR turns it into an AND.

p ∨ q ≡ p ∧ q

"NOT (pizza OR pasta)"
≡ "NOT pizza AND NOT pasta"

p ∧ q ≡ p ∨ q

"NOT (A and B)"
≡ "NOT A or NOT B"

Absorption

Logic shortcut

p∧(p∨q) ≡ p
p∨(p∧q) ≡ p
Eng: "I go to work, OR (I go to work AND drive)" → I go to work.

Involution

Double negation

p ≡ p
Eng: "I am NOT NOT hungry" → I am hungry.

Identity

Absolute states

p∨f ≡ p p∧t ≡ p p∨t ≡ t p∧f ≡ f

Complement

Opposite interaction

p∨p ≡ t p∧p ≡ f f ≡ t t ≡ f

02. Duality & Replacement

These mechanics justify multi-step proofs. Duality involves strict swaps, while Replacement allows you to substitute logically equivalent formulas inside larger equations.

Duality Principle

If two propositions are logically equivalent, their duals are also logically equivalent.

Required Swaps

Example Transformation

(p ∧ q) ∨ p → (p ∨ q) ∧ p

(p ∨ f) ∧ q → (p ∧ t) ∨ q

Replacement Rule

Substituting a logically equivalent proposition for another does not alter the truth value.

04. Equivalence vs. Implication

Understand the difference between strong equivalence (identical truth values) and logical implication (directional truth).

Equivalence P ≡ Q

  • Propositions have identical truth values for every component set.
  • The biconditional statement P ↔ Q is a tautology.
Example Equation p ∧ q ≡ p ∨ q

Implication P ⇒ Q

  • Whenever P is true, Q is also true.
  • The conditional statement P → Q is a tautology.
  • Converse does not apply. Q may be true when P is false.
Example Equation q ⇒ (p ∨ q)

Defining the scope of the variable substitution.
