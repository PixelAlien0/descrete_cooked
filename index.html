01. The 9 Laws

Fundamental algebraic manipulation operations. You don't need to memorize the complex formulas right away; you need to understand what they do conceptually.

Idempotent

Repeated execution

p∧p ≡ p
p∨p ≡ p
Eng: "It is raining AND it is raining" → It is raining.

Commutative

Order is irrelevant

p∧q ≡ q∧p
p∨q ≡ q∨p
p↔q ≡ q↔p
Eng: "I have a cat AND a dog" ≡ "I have a dog AND a cat"

Associative

Parenthesis shift

(p∧q)∧r ≡ p∧(q∧r)
(p∨q)∨r ≡ p∨(q∨r)
Eng: "Wash car AND mow lawn, AND buy groceries" ≡ "Wash car, AND mow lawn AND buy groceries"

Distributive

Distribute over

p∧(q∨r) ≡ (p∧q)∨(p∧r)
p∨(q∧r) ≡ (p∨q)∧(p∨r)
Eng: "I will order a burger AND (either fries OR salad)"
Crucial Exam Topic

De Morgan's

Invert and flip. Negating a grouped AND turns it into an OR.

p ∨ q ≡ p ∧ q

"NOT (pizza OR pasta)"
≡ "NOT pizza AND NOT pasta"

p ∧ q ≡ p ∨ q

"NOT (A and B)"
≡ "NOT A or NOT B"

Absorption

Logic shortcut

p∧(p∨q) ≡ p
p∨(p∧q) ≡ p
Eng: "I go to work, OR (I go to work AND drive)" → I go to work.

Involution

Double negation

p ≡ p
Eng: "I am NOT NOT hungry" → I am hungry.

Identity

Absolute states

p∨f ≡ p p∧t ≡ p p∨t ≡ t p∧f ≡ f

Complement

Opposite interaction

p∨p ≡ t p∧p ≡ f f ≡ t t ≡ f

02. Core Principles

Mechanical operations for substitution and equivalence.

Duality Principle

To find the dual of a compound proposition, execute strict mechanical swaps.

Golden Rule: If two propositions are logically equivalent, their duals are also logically equivalent.

Required Structural Swaps:

∧ ∨
t f

Dual Transformation 01

(p ∧ q) ∨ p  →  (p ∨ q) ∧ p

Dual Transformation 02

(p ∨ f) ∧ q  →  (p ∧ t) ∨ q

Replacement Rule

Substituting a logically equivalent proposition for another in a compound proposition does not alter the ultimate truth value.

p ∧ t Complement

03. Conditionals

Structural permutations of a basic proposition.

Base Proposition

p → q

"If Jack plays his guitar, then Sara will sing."

Converse

q → p

"If Sara will sing, then Jack plays his guitar."

Inverse

p → q

"If Jack doesn't play his guitar, then Sara won't sing."

Contrapositive

q → p

"If Sara won't sing, then Jack doesn't play his guitar."

Verification Matrix

Observe equivalent truth values.

p q p→q q→p p→q q→p
T T T T T T
T F F T T F
F T T F F T
F F T T T T

A conditional & its contrapositive share the exact same truth values. They are logically equivalent.

04. Equivalence vs. Implication

Differentiating strict logic states.

Logical Equivalence P ≡ Q

  • Two propositions have identical truth values for every possible set of components.
  • The biconditional statement P ↔ Q is a tautology (always true).
Example Equation
p ∧ q ≡ p ∨ q

Logical Implication P ⇒ Q

  • Whenever P is true, then Q is also true. (Q is never false when P is true).
  • The conditional statement P → Q is a tautology.
  • Warning: The converse does not apply. Q may be true when P is false.
Example Equation
q ⇒ (p ∨ q)

05. Argument Validity

Proving argument structures via tautology.

Validation Formula

An argument is VALID if the conjunction (AND) of the premises logically implies the conclusion. If the premises are true, the conclusion cannot be false.

(P1 ∧ P2 ∧ ... Pn) → Q Must = Tautology

"If you insulted Bob, then I'll never speak to you again. You insulted Bob so I'll never speak to you again."

06. Core Predicates

Extending logic to describe properties and individuals.

1. Predicates

Represents a property, trait, or relationship. Use Uppercase Letters.

T(x) = "is Tall"
L(x,y) = "Loves"

2. Variables

Placeholders for templates. Lowercase from end of alphabet.

x, y, z

3. Constants

Specific subjects. Lowercase from start/middle of alphabet.

m = Mary
b = Bill

07. Quantifiers

Universal Quantifier

∀

Indicates the statement is true for every substitution.

  • Keywords: All, every, each, any, for all.
  • Pairing: Usually paired with the conditional (→).

Translation Example

"All humans are mortal."

∀x [H(x) → M(x)]

Existential Quantifier

∃

Indicates the statement is true for at least one substitution.

  • Keywords: Some, there exists, at least one.
  • Pairing: Usually paired with logical AND (∧).

Translation Example

"Some rats are grey."

∃x [R(x) ∧ G(x)]
