The 9 Propositional Laws

Fundamental rules for manipulating and simplifying logical statements.

Idempotent

Applying same operation repeatedly does not change it.

p∧p ≡ p
p∨p ≡ p
"It is raining AND it is raining" → It is raining.

Commutative

The order of propositions does not change the outcome.

p∧q ≡ q∧p
p∨q ≡ q∨p
p↔q ≡ q↔p
"I have a cat AND a dog" ≡ "I have a dog AND a cat"

Associative

Parenthesis grouping doesn't matter for chains of same operators.

(p∧q)∧r ≡ p∧(q∧r)
(p∨q)∨r ≡ p∨(q∨r)
"Wash car AND mow lawn, AND buy groceries" ≡ "Wash car, AND mow lawn AND buy groceries"

Distributive

Distribute AND over OR, or OR over AND.

p∧(q∨r) ≡ (p∧q)∨(p∧r)
p∨(q∧r) ≡ (p∨q)∧(p∨r)
"I will order a burger AND (either fries OR salad)"
Crucial

De Morgan's Laws

Negating a grouped AND turns it into an OR. Negating a grouped OR turns it into an AND.

p ∨ q ≡ p ∧ q

"NOT (pizza OR pasta)"
≡ "NOT pizza AND NOT pasta"

p ∧ q ≡ p ∨ q

"NOT (A and B)"
≡ "NOT A or NOT B"

Absorption

Shortcuts! p absorbs compounds containing p.

p∧(p∨q) ≡ p
p∨(p∧q) ≡ p
"I go to work, OR (I go to work AND drive)" → I go to work.

Involution

Double negation. Two negatives cancel out.

p ≡ p
"I am NOT NOT hungry" → I am hungry.

Identity & Domination

Combining p with True (t) or False (f).

p∨f ≡ p p∧t ≡ p p∨t ≡ t p∧f ≡ f

Complement

Relationship between proposition and its exact opposite.

p∨p ≡ t p∧p ≡ f f ≡ t t ≡ f

Core Principles

The mechanical rules that govern logical equivalence.

The Duality Principle

To find the dual of a compound proposition, you execute strict mechanical swaps.

Golden Rule: If two propositions are logically equivalent, their duals are also logically equivalent.

The Required Swaps:

∧ ∨
t f

Example 1

(p ∧ q) ∨ p  →  (p ∨ q) ∧ p

Example 2

(p ∨ f) ∧ q  →  (p ∧ t) ∨ q

Replacement Rule

Substituting a logically equivalent proposition for another in a compound proposition does not alter the ultimate truth value.

Equivalence vs. Implication

Logical Equivalence P ≡ Q

  • Two propositions have identical truth values for every possible set of components.
  • It means the biconditional statement P ↔ Q is a tautology (always true).
  • Example p ∧ q ≡ p ∨ q

Logical Implication P ⇒ Q

  • Whenever P is true, then Q is also true. (Q is never false when P is true).
  • It means the conditional statement P → Q is a tautology.
  • Warning: The converse does not apply. Q may be true when P is false.
  • Example q ⇒ (p ∨ q)

Valid Arguments Validation

Validation Formula

An argument is VALID if the conjunction (AND) of the premises logically implies the conclusion. If the premises are true, the conclusion cannot be false.

(P1 ∧ P2 ∧ ... Pn) → Q Must = Tautology
