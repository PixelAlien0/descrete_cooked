1. Algebra of Propositions (The 9 Core Laws)

Think of these laws as the "rules of the game" for logical statements, similar to standard algebra. We use $t$ for an absolute True and $f$ for an absolute False.

Idempotent Laws

Applying the same operation to a single proposition repeatedly does not change it.

$p \wedge p \equiv p$
$p \vee p \equiv p$
Ex: Saying "It is raining AND it is raining" is the exact same as just saying "It is raining".

Commutative Laws

The order in which you present the propositions does not change the outcome.

$p \wedge q \equiv q \wedge p$
$p \vee q \equiv q \vee p$
Ex: "I have a cat AND I have a dog" is the exact same as "I have a dog AND I have a cat".

Associative Laws

When you have a chain of the exact same operators, how you group them with parentheses doesn't matter.

$(p \wedge q) \wedge r \equiv p \wedge (q \wedge r)$
$(p \vee q) \vee r \equiv p \vee (q \vee r)$
Ex: "I will (wash the car AND mow the lawn), AND buy groceries" is the same as "I will wash the car, AND (mow the lawn AND buy groceries)".

Distributive Laws

Works like algebra: $x(y+z) = xy + xz$. You distribute an AND over an OR, or vice versa.

$p \wedge (q \vee r) \equiv (p \wedge q) \vee (p \wedge r)$
$p \vee (q \wedge r) \equiv (p \vee q) \wedge (p \vee r)$
Ex: "I will order a burger AND (get fries OR a salad)" means you get a burger and fries, OR a burger and a salad.

De Morgan's Laws

When you distribute a NOT ($\sim$ or $\overline{p}$) into parentheses, AND flips to OR, and OR flips to AND.

$\overline{p \vee q} \equiv \overline{p} \wedge \overline{q}$
$\overline{p \wedge q} \equiv \overline{p} \vee \overline{q}$
Ex: "It is NOT true that I will eat pizza OR pasta" means "I will NOT eat pizza AND I will NOT eat pasta".

Complement Laws

Defines the relationship between a proposition and its exact opposite.

$p \vee \overline{p} \equiv t$
$p \wedge \overline{p} \equiv f$
Ex: Law of Excluded Middle: A coin flip will result in Heads OR it will NOT result in Heads. One must be True ($t$).

Absorption Laws

Shortcuts: If $p$ is paired with a compound statement containing both $p$ and $q$, the $p$ "absorbs" the rest.

$p \wedge (p \vee q) \equiv p$
$p \vee (p \wedge q) \equiv p$
Ex: "I go to work, OR (I go to work AND drive a car)" simplifies to just "I go to work".

Identity (Domination) Laws

What happens when you combine an unknown ($p$) with an absolute True ($t$) or False ($f$).

$p \vee f \equiv p$  |  $p \vee t \equiv t$
$p \wedge t \equiv p$  |  $p \wedge f \equiv f$
Ex: "My name is John ($p$) AND the sun is a star ($t$)" is only true if my name is actually John.

Involution Law

Double Negation: Two negatives cancel each other out entirely.

$\overline{\overline{p}} \equiv p$
Ex: "It is NOT true that I am NOT going" simply means "I am going".

2. Duality & Replacement

The Duality Principle

Given any compound proposition involving only $\wedge$ and $\vee$, you can find its dual by replacing $\wedge$ with $\vee$ (and vice versa), and $t$ with $f$ (and vice versa).

Example: The dual of $(p \vee f) \wedge q$ is $(p \wedge t) \vee q$.

4. Arguments & Validity

An argument is valid if the conjunction (ANDing) of all the premises logically implies the conclusion.

$(P_1 \wedge P_2 \wedge \dots \wedge P_n) \rightarrow Q \text{ is a Tautology.}$

5. Logical Equivalence ($P \equiv Q$)

Two propositions $P$ and $Q$ are logically equivalent ($P \equiv Q$) if and only if the biconditional statement $P \leftrightarrow Q$ is a tautology (always true).

6. Logical Implication ($P \Rightarrow Q$)

'$P \Rightarrow Q$' and '$P \rightarrow Q$ is a tautology' mean exactly the same thing. The truth of $P$ guarantees the truth of $Q$.
